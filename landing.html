Pain is an important part of being human, but we don’t have to live with it on a daily basis. Pain has evolved with our biology as a useful tool to keep us safe and free from harm. It teaches us, in the form of a physically felt experience, what in our outer world is safe and what is dangerous. Pain a response to a signal or queue from our environment that something in which we’re engaging with may be threatening to our safety. Experiencing pain (for example: when you touch a hot pan) allows us to learn how to behave safely when the next time comes around.
Pain and the Brain
The experience of pain is a dynamic between a person and their environment, and within this dynamic, the brain plays a very important role. As mentioned earlier, the pain pathways in the brain are developmentally important to human survival and safety, however, chronic pain can be an interpretation of the brain that our environment is fundamentally dangerous or threatening to us. This also is not purely limited to your physical environment, but your emotional and social environment can also set off stress and eventually pain mechanisms in the body.

However, sometimes the brain will misinterpret the environment, or the body will misinterpret the brain. Sometimes, we are fed the same information over and over again from our environment that we take it to be true. Whatever it is, the happenings of our lives don’t simply pass us by. Everything that you interact with gets processed by the brain and integrated into your biology. We call these things “happenings” because they come and go. Some last longer than others, and some come and leave within a matter of minutes or seconds. For example, if you and your friend are walking down the street, and you like admiring trees but your friend likes admiring buildings, while you are going for the same walk, the trees last longer in your awareness then the buildings do, and vice versa for your friend. Certain experiences can be more sticky in your awareness than others, all of this is natural and what it is can vary from person to person.
How long an object "sticks" in our memory often depends more on us than the object
Predictive Coding
Here's an example: let's say you twist your ankle and experience bad pain, the pain is very intense and makes a firm impression on your awareness. The thought and feeling of pain becomes sticky in your brain. From there on our, you move cautiously through your life, as to not trigger the pain in your ankle. After a few weeks, your ankle has healed! However, you have made it a practice to move carefully, and your brain has become accustomed to expecting and avoiding pain. While the source of the pain may have resolved itself, your brain is not able to tell the difference between the signal of expecting pain and an external signal that causes such pain. Since the pain pathway is already paved, and you send a lot of energy in and around that pathway, even with the good intention to NOT trigger pain, the road maintenance is top notch because the impression is so sticky.
Flow States
The best way to pave a new road in the brain is to be fully immersed in the task you have at hand, and ideally, they are simple and repetitive motions that are stimulating, but not too challenging or too dull. Ideally, this is something that you enjoy doing such as drawing, driving, or gardening, but this state of immersive flow can also be accessed through activities in your daily life like chopping vegetables or washing dishes. These are helpful practices because 1. They involve the body and keep you in the body 2. The repetition acts like a metronome, locking you in time as the moment unfolds. These practices act like a container, holding you in time at the brink of the present as it is unfolding. As you are in this flow, the moment has a newness and a freshness to it, and since you are locked in the moment, the usual patterns of expectation and prediction in your brain are not firing.
Reconnecting with Nature
Setting the intention and time to connect with nature is an extremely fruitful practice. While it is amazing to have a vast mountainous range with tall trees and running rivers, nature is in fact all around us! It is equally effective to set aside 15 undistracted minutes (as often as you can) to sit on your front steps or balcony and listen to the birds, watch the trees sway, watch the squirrels run about, or watch the little spider on your railing. Even if you are in a big city, you can take some time to sit with the little weed growing through the sidewalk, or look out your window at the vast horizon of land and buildings. Of course, the bustling city life is still a work of nature! The fruits of this practice take on a wide range. If you are totally not engaged, but still sit outside, the sounds of birds chirping calm the body and signal that it is safe, since birds don't chirp and chatter when there is a predator around. However, after building a practice of sitting outside, you may start to realize that birds speak many languages. When the birds are in danger, they make a sound some call the bird “alarm”, and this sounds very different from the usual chitter. While it may seem like an irrelevant thing to learn, sitting in nature and listening to the language of your local birds is the perfect gateway into knitting a deep connection with nature. For example, maybe you're at work or on your lunch break, and hear the familiar danger-call (bird alarm) that the birds by your house make. You recognise it! This small awareness pulls you out of the stress and intensity of being a working human, and you remember that there are other beings out there also going through their day of gathering berries and hiding from predators. These types of small practices, like simply sitting out in nature can make happiness and inner clarity (healing) habitual. You return to work, but with more perspective and understanding that you and others that you work with all similarly emerged from the energy of the sun, water, earth, and forces of nature. Connection with nature encourages a more lighthearted perspective.

Pain is an emotional experience and can be accompanied with sleep issues, anxiety, and depression. The healing effect of nature on the body can be showcased for instance from the forest therapy study, where a pool of individuals coping with chronic pain that was “present for three months or more in at least five tender points" were placed in the program. During this two day program, participants engaged in indoor and outdoor activities at the camp. The results displayed “significant increase in some measures of heart rate variability and an increase in immune competence as indicated by natural killer cell activity. Participants in the forest therapy group also reported significant decreases in pain and depression”. Being in the forest physically relaxes your body and soothes your anxiety as you immerse yourself in the natural surroundings. Being in this mindful place and doing an activity, something as simple as a stretch can reduce the intensity with which you perceive pain.
